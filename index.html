Jesus on Wealth and Possession

Perhaps one of the most challenging aspects of Jesus' teaching was his perspective on wealth. He did not view money as inherently evil, but rather as a rival to God for the human heart. He famously stated: "No one can serve two masters... You cannot serve both God and Money" (Matthew 6:24).

For Jesus, wealth often acted as a barrier to the "narrow gate" of spiritual liberation. He challenged the rich young ruler to sell everything and give to the poor, not as a punishment, but as a way to find true treasure. He viewed wealth not as something to be accumulated for security or status, but as a resource for radical generosity.

What is the Church?

Beyond the stone walls and towering spires, the word "Church" originates from the Greek ekklesia, meaning a "called-out assembly." In its purest theological sense, the Church is not a building or a political entity, but a community of people united by faith and a shared commitment to the Gospel.

It is described as the Body of Christ, intended to be a living vessel of God's love, mercy, and justice on Earth. However, throughout the centuries, the definition has evolved to include the Institution‚Äîthe organized structure of hierarchy, laws, and traditions that manage the global community of believers.

The duality of the Church lies here: it is both a spiritual fellowship and a human institution, susceptible to the same hunger for power that Jesus once warned against.

Historical Accumulation

When the Institution Mirrored the Empire

I

The Feudal Landowner

By the High Middle Ages, the Church was the largest landowner in Europe. Through tithes and bequests, monasteries amassed immense wealth in land and production, often wielding more economic power than local kings.

II

Renaissance Splendor

The papacy became a patron of the arts, commissioning grand works like St. Peter's Basilica. To fund this, the Church monetized spiritual grace through the sale of "indulgences," a practice that sparked the Protestant Reformation.

III

Imperial Expansion

During the colonial era, the Church expanded alongside empires into the Americas and Asia. Missions often controlled vast resources and territories, intertwining the mission of evangelization with material extraction.

Preserving Knowledge & Healing the Sick

Wealth as a Social Safety Net

However, it is historically inaccurate to view Church wealth solely as corruption. For centuries, before the existence of modern governments and welfare states, the Church was the primary provider of social services in the Western world.

The immense resources collected through tithes were often redistributed to fund the first hospitals (like the H√¥tel-Dieu in Paris), orphanages, and almshouses. Monasteries served not only as spiritual retreats but as centers of agricultural innovation and food distribution during famines.

The Enduring Paradox

"The history of the Church cannot be reduced to a single narrative of corruption or sanctity. It is, instead, a profound paradox."

While the institution has at times succumbed to the very temptations of power that Jesus warned against, it has also served as the primary vessel for his message of love and service to endure through millennia. The wealth that built cathedrals also built hospitals; the authority that waged wars also mediated peace.

Ultimately, the tension between faith and power is not a problem that was solved in the past, but a constant balance to be maintained in the present. It stands as a reminder that the true Church exists not in gold or stone, but in the continual, imperfect effort to align human institutions with divine ideals.
